1. The Book in Two Minutes

Stefanini was deputy CIO of an Italian fund-of-funds firm; his book is a practitioner's tour of the strategies hedge funds actually run. It is not a recipe book — the author explicitly says "strategy is easy, execution is hard" — but it is the cleanest taxonomy you will find. Every chapter follows the same eight-part skeleton, and that skeleton is exactly what a strategy builder needs as input fields.

Stefanini's per-chapter skeleton

History

How the strategy emerged and what it was originally designed to exploit.

Theory

The economic or structural inefficiency being captured.

Securities & Market Size

Instruments used; depth and capacity of the market.

Hedging & Derivatives

How directional risk is neutralized.

Trading Examples

Concrete past trades with P&L mechanics.

Liquidity

Lockups, holding period, slippage profile.

Leverage

Typical gross/net leverage used.

Risks & Risk Management

Failure modes, hedges, exposure caps.

3. The Core Insight: Every Strategy Is a Stack of Decisions

Read Stefanini end-to-end and one pattern dominates: a hedge fund strategy is not an "idea", it is a sequence of seven or eight decisions stacked on top of each other. Long/short equity and merger arbitrage look unrelated until you notice both answer the same questions — what universe? what edge? how to enter? how to hedge? how to size? when to exit? what blows it up? how to verify?

A good no-code builder is therefore not a blank canvas. It is a structured form whose fields are exactly those decisions, plus a templates layer (one template per chapter of the book) and a backtest engine that runs the resulting configuration over historical data.

Design corollary. If your UI lets a user skip any of the eight layers, the resulting strategy will leak somewhere — usually as unmodeled risk or unrealistic fills. The builder should require all eight, even if defaults are sensible.

4. The 8-Layer Strategy Framework

This is the universal template. Every strategy in the book — and every strategy a user will build — can be expressed by filling in these eight layers.

1Universe
2Thesis
3Signal
4Execution
5Risk
6Liquidity
7Backtest
8Review & Iterate — the loop back into Layer 2
1

Universe Layer — "What can I trade?"

Defines the investable set: asset class, geography, market cap, liquidity floor, sector, instrument type.

Book mappings: Long/short equity → US large-caps; Merger arb → announced deals; Managed futures → liquid global futures.
2

Thesis Layer — "What inefficiency am I capturing?"

The economic story. Mispricing? Risk premium? Behavioral bias? Structural flow? Without an articulated thesis, a strategy is data-mined.

Book mappings: Convertible arb → embedded option underpriced; Distressed → recovery > market price; Trend-following → serial correlation in prices.
3

Signal Layer — "How do I know when to act?"

The rule that translates data into a decision. Specifies entry, exit, and rebalancing.

Examples: 12-1 momentum percentile; pair z-score > 2; deal spread > 3%; implied vs realized vol gap.
4

Execution Layer — "How do I express the trade?"

Long-only, long/short, paired, hedged with derivatives, leveraged. Position sizing (equal-weight, risk-parity, signal-weighted). Order type and slippage assumption.

5

Risk Layer — "What kills this strategy?"

Per-name caps, sector caps, gross/net leverage limits, stop-loss, factor exposure limits.

Failure modes: deal break (merger arb), short squeeze, liquidity gap (distressed), credit spread blowout (convert arb), regime shift (trend).
6

Liquidity Layer — "How fast can I get in and out?"

Holding period, capacity, transaction cost model. Stefanini's "capacity" discussion is why the largest hedge funds are smaller than the largest mutual funds.

7

Backtest Layer — "Has this ever worked?"

Historical simulation. Core metrics only at MVP. Run on in-sample, always present out-of-sample alongside in v2.

8

Review & Iterate Layer — "What broke, and why?"

Attribution by signal, by name, by regime. The loop that turns a backtest into a discipline.

6. Backtesting — Core Essentials

MetricFormula / DefinitionWhy it matters
Total Return(end equity / start equity) − 1Headline P&L.
CAGR(1 + total return)^(1/years) − 1Time-normalized return.
Volatilitystdev(daily returns) × √252Risk dimension.
Sharpe Ratio(CAGR − rf) / volatilityRisk-adjusted return; Stefanini's primary yardstick.
Max Drawdownmax peak-to-trough declineWhat a user actually has to live through.
Win Ratewinning trades / total tradesBehavioral comfort.
Exposure & Turnoveravg gross & net; annualized turnoverSanity-checks capacity and TC realism.

Assumptions baked into the MVP engine

Slippage

Fixed bps per trade, user-editable. Defaults: 5 bps equities, 1 bps futures, 25 bps small-caps.

Commissions

Flat per-share or per-trade, user-editable.

Borrow cost

Annualized rate applied to short notional. Default 50 bps for liquid names.

Funding cost

Cash rate on uninvested capital.

Fills

Next-bar open; market-on-close for daily strategies.

Universe rebuild

Point-in-time membership when survivorship-bias-free data is available.

Walk-forward, true OOS, Monte Carlo, regime conditioning, lookahead detection are roadmap v2. Pro tier surfaces them as locked-with-tooltip items.

7. Tiered UX: One Product, Three Experiences

Same engine and data model underneath. Tiers gate which layers are user-exposed versus auto-filled with sane defaults.

Starter

Retail. Comfortable with stocks; not with code or quant jargon.
  • Pick a template (e.g. "Trend-Following Stocks").
  • Drag sliders for 3–5 parameters.
  • Plain-English risk controls.
  • One-click backtest, 5 core metrics, equity curve.
  • Plain-English summary.

Builder

Prosumer. Knows Sharpe and drawdown; doesn't want to code.
  • All Starter features.
  • Per-layer editing of all 8 framework layers.
  • Block-based signal composer.
  • Custom universes.
  • Vol-target, risk-parity sizing.
  • Full metric panel; save, fork, share.

Pro

Quant pro. Wants Python/DSL and API access.
  • Strategy DSL (YAML) + Python SDK.
  • Raw data tables, intraday bars.
  • Walk-forward, OOS, Monte Carlo (v2).
  • Factor-attribution & regime analysis.
  • REST/Webhook API.
  • Custom slippage and TC models.

8. Fintech Tool — Technical Specification

8.1 Product summary

A web application that lets users construct quantitative investment strategies via a tiered no-code/low-code interface, backtest them on historical market data, and review results. Strategies are first-class documents: versioned, forkable, shareable, exportable.

8.4 Anti-patterns to prevent (lessons from Stefanini)

"Strategy is easy, execution is hard." The product should actively guard against the most common ways users mislead themselves.
  • Survivorship bias. Refuse to backtest equity strategies against current-membership universes; require point-in-time.
  • Lookahead bias. Force signals to use data with a configurable lag.
  • Overfitting. Soft-warn when a strategy has many parameters per year of data.
  • Capacity ignorance. Show estimated max AUM at which slippage erodes ≥50% of edge.
  • Currency bias. Force a base currency.
  • Cherry-pick. Default backtest window is full available history.
